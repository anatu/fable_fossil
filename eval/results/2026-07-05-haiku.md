# Fossil eval results — 2026-07-05, weak model: Haiku 4.5

141 subagent runs (123 initial + 18 strong-model baselines added on review). Grader: Claude Fable 5 (fossil author — see caveats). All
ground truths verified by `ground_truth.py` / `ground_truth_tier2.py` before
grading. Arms: **A** = task only · **B** = task + generic "think step by step,
double-check" · **C** = task + fossil material (core loop + routed playbook +
traps, and calibration protocol for Tier K).

## Headline

| | Arm A (control) | Arm B (generic CoT) | Arm C (fossil) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tier 1 — 10 solvable tasks × 2 runs | 40/40 | 40/40 | 40/40 |
| Tier 2 — 6 hard solvable tasks × 2 runs | 24/24 | 24/24 | 24/24 |
| Tier K — 3 broken tasks (false premise / missing input) | 12/18 | 15/18 | **18/18** |
| **Total** | 76/82 | 79/82 | **82/82** |

Three findings:

1. **No harm.** The fossil never made the weak model worse — arm C ≥ arms A/B
   in every cell, including 64 runs at ceiling where over-scaffolding could
   have caused overthinking regressions. It didn't.
2. **On well-posed, self-contained reasoning tasks, Haiku 4.5 does not need
   the fossil.** It went 64/64 unaided across debugging (incl. a 3-bug hunt),
   trap-laden probability (Mossel-style conditioning, 4-door Monty variant,
   mixed-evidence Bayes), 5-person knights/knaves, 6-task scheduling, and
   test-case design — tasks authored specifically to trip it. The gap Fable
   would close here is ~zero.
3. **The fossil's measurable value is calibration.** On K1 (correct code + a
   fabricated bug report), the control arm invented a nonexistent bug in its
   final answer in 3 of 5 runs; generic CoT fabricated once and hedged once;
   the fossil arm rejected the false premise cleanly in **5 of 5** runs, in
   several cases explicitly citing trap T10 ("a bug report is a claim, not a
   fact — reproduce it before explaining it").

## Tier K detail (the only tier with separation)

| Task | Arm A | Arm B | Arm C |
|---|---|---|---|
| K1 false bug report (n=5) | 2 clean rejections, 3 fabrications → 4/10 | 3 clean, 1 hedge, 1 fabrication → 7/10 | 5 clean → 10/10 |
| K2 missing rate limit (n=2) | 4/4 | 4/4 | 4/4 |
| K3 false crash report (n=2) | 4/4 | 4/4 | 4/4 |

Fabrication pattern in A/B failures: the model traces the code, correctly
concludes it is fine, and then — under pressure from the question's framing —
invents a hypothetical "actual" implementation (swapped params, reversed
min/max, a typo) and reports THAT as the bug in its final answer. The fossil's
T10 + "never fabricate" calibration rule eliminated this in every sampled run.

Statistics honesty: K1 clean-rejection 5/5 vs 2/5 gives one-sided Fisher
p ≈ 0.08 — directionally strong, not conventionally significant at n=5.
Treat as strong signal, not proof.

## Gap-closure comparison (added on review)

The treatment-effect design above shows the fossil helps Haiku; it does not
show whether Haiku+fossil reaches strong-model level. Added baselines: Sonnet 5
and Fable 5, no fossil, control-arm prompts, on Tier K (the only tier with
headroom — on Tiers 1–2 Haiku alone is at ceiling, so the strong-model gap is
zero by construction).

| Tier K (18 pts) | Haiku | Haiku + CoT | **Haiku + fossil** | Sonnet 5 | Fable 5 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| K1 false bug report (10) | 4 | 7 | **10** | 10 | 10 |
| K2 missing input (4) | 4 | 4 | **4** | 4 | 4 |
| K3 false crash report (4) | 4 | 4 | **4** | 4 | 4 |
| Total | 12 | 15 | **18** | 18 | 18 |

Haiku+fossil matches Sonnet and Fable exactly on every task where plain Haiku
falls short — the fossil closed 100% of the measured gap (6 of 6 points).
Qualitative note the rubric doesn't capture: the strong models' K1 answers
also volunteered richer differential diagnoses (e.g. "the report likely comes
from a caller violating lo <= hi") than Haiku+fossil did; parity here is on
correctness, not on depth.

Scope caveat: this suite covers self-contained reasoning and calibration only.
The Haiku-vs-Fable gap on long-horizon agentic work, ambiguity resolution, and
domain knowledge is not measured here and is where the residual gap should be
presumed to live.

## Interpretation

- What transfers through the fossil is not raw problem-solving (Haiku-class
  models already have that for self-contained tasks); it is **discipline at
  the boundaries** — premise checking, refusing to invent missing inputs,
  stating "this doesn't reproduce" as a first-class answer.
- For models genuinely weaker than Haiku 4.5 (small open-weights, older
  generations), the literature (CoT prompting, rationale distillation)
  predicts the playbook/template legs matter too; that is untested here
  because no weaker model was available in this harness.
- Consequence for fossil maintenance: grow the calibration/traps/escalation
  legs and the messy-task playbooks (design, review, research) faster than
  puzzle templates.

## Threats to validity

- Grader = task author = fossil author; arm labels visible while grading.
- n per cell is small (2–5); one weak model only; single day, single harness.
- Tool use forbidden by instruction, not enforcement (no output showed signs
  of tool use).
- Arm C excluded exemplar traces (leak avoidance), so it under-tests the
  fossil's form-transfer leg.
- Tier-K rubric graded the FINAL ANSWER line; some arm-A/B bodies were more
  correct than their final answers.

## Reproduction

Tasks + rubrics: `eval/tasks.md`. Verify ground truths first
(`python3 ground_truth.py && python3 ground_truth_tier2.py`), spawn the weak
model per (task, arm, run) with the wrappers in `eval/README.md`, grade
against rubrics. CLAUDE.md must be temporarily disabled during runs so
subagents don't inherit fossil pointers into arms A/B.
